# Host A — The Frustrated App Wrestler (Raw Transcript)

## Section 1 — Warm-up and background

**Mike:** Thanks for jumping on. To start easy, tell me a bit about your hosting situation. How many properties, where, how long?

**Host A:** Sure. So it's one place. Just one. It's in the city, kind of a walkable neighborhood, and I've been doing it for almost two years now. I keep thinking I'll add a second unit but honestly, with how this one's been going, I'm in no hurry.

**Mike:** Which booking platforms are you on?

**Host A:** Just Airbnb. I looked at putting it on VRBO at one point but I couldn't deal with managing two calendars on top of everything else. Airbnb's where my bookings come from, so I just stayed there.

**Mike:** Walk me through your operating model. Solo, cleaner, property manager?

**Host A:** Mostly solo. I have a cleaner who comes between guests but I'm the one doing everything else. Messages, pricing, the whole lock situation, all of it. No property manager, no co-host, none of that. It's just me on my phone when something goes wrong.

**Mike:** Got it. And your lock setup today?

**Host A:** It's a Schlage Encode. Just the one, on the front door. I put it in maybe a year and a half ago, somewhere around there. I had a Wi-Fi bridge thing plugged in too, because the Wi-Fi situation in the unit is kind of split. There's a 2.4 gig network and a 5 gig and the lock is fussy about which one it's on. That part has been a nightmare honestly.

**Mike:** Walk me through how front-door access works for a typical booking, beginning to end. Don't shortcut.

**Host A:** Okay so. Guest books on Airbnb. I get the notification. I go into the Schlage app and I create a code for them, usually the last four of their phone number or something easy, and I set the schedule for their check-in to checkout. Then I copy that code into my Airbnb message template and send it to them with the check-in instructions. Day of, I'm kind of just refreshing the Schlage app to see if it shows the code is on the lock. Sometimes it does, sometimes it just says nothing, and I sit there going, did it sync, did it not. Guest arrives, hopefully it works, and after checkout I go back in and delete the code. The cleaner has her own permanent code, which I know I should rotate but I don't.

## Section 2 — Problem validation

**Mike:** Tell me about the last time front-door access didn't go smoothly.

**Host A:** Oh god. Okay so, the lock said 85 percent battery in the app. Eighty-five. The guests show up and the keypad won't light up at all. Like, dead. They're texting me from the front step. I'm checking the app and it still says 85 percent. So I had to walk them through opening the battery compartment and putting new batteries in themselves, which, you know, that's not what they signed up for.

**Mike:** How'd you find out something was wrong? Guest, or you?

**Host A:** Guest. One hundred percent guest. I had no idea anything was off until I got the message. That's the part that kills me, the app told me everything was fine.

**Mike:** What did you do once you knew, in order?

**Host A:** First I panicked for like two minutes. Then I tried to remote unlock from the app but that didn't work either because the lock was offline at that point. Then I called the guests and walked them through the battery thing. I keep spare batteries in a drawer inside but obviously they couldn't get to the drawer because they couldn't get in. Luckily there was a corner store half a block away. Whole thing took maybe forty minutes from their first text to them being inside.

**Mike:** What did that cost you?

**Host A:** Four-star review. They were nice about it in the message but the stars don't lie. And I comped them like thirty bucks for the batteries and the trouble. The bigger cost is honestly that I don't sleep well the night before a check-in anymore. I just wait for my phone to buzz.

**Mike:** Set aside the big failures. On a normal week, what's the most annoying part?

**Host A:** Honestly? Not knowing. Like, the app shows me a status but I don't trust the status anymore. So even on a normal Friday I'm refreshing the app three times before a guest arrives and I still don't actually know if the code is on the lock until they tell me they got in. It's this low-level dread thing.

**Mike:** How do you handle codes for cleaners and maintenance?

**Host A:** Cleaner has a permanent code. Has had it since I onboarded her. I know rotating it is a thing you're supposed to do but every time I think about doing it I think about what happens if she shows up and the code doesn't work and I'm at my actual job, and I just leave it alone. Maintenance, if a plumber or whatever comes, I set a one-time code and delete it the same day. That part I'm okay with.

**Mike:** Hypothetical, if you knew there was a 50% chance the lock would fail at tonight's check-in, what would you actually do?

**Host A:** I'd drive over. I would just go. I'd put fresh batteries in myself, I'd test the code on the keypad with my own thumb, and I'd probably sit in the unit until the guest got there. Because the alternative is the 40-minute disaster I just described, and that's not worth whatever I'd be doing instead that night.

## Section 3 — Concept presentation

**Mike:** Okay, I'm going to read you a product concept and I want your honest reaction. (reads C-pre concept verbatim)

**Host A:** Wait, can I ask a thing first? When you say it checks if the code is "physically present on the lock," does that mean it's actually pinging the lock, or is it just checking what the app thinks should be there?

**Mike:** Actually pinging the lock. The check requires the lock to be online and responsive, and the system confirms the code exists on the device itself.

**Host A:** Okay. Okay, that's a real check then. Because the app today is basically lying to me half the time. If this thing is actually talking to the lock and confirming what's on it, that's different.

## Section 4 — Solution comprehension

**Mike:** In your own words, what is this product?

**Host A:** It's a pre-flight check for your lock. Like before the guest shows up it makes sure everything is actually going to work, and it tells you if something's broken so you can fix it before the guest is standing outside texting you. And then it handles the codes for you on top, so you don't have to remember to add and delete them.

**Mike:** What problem do you think it's meant to solve? And what's it NOT trying to solve?

**Host A:** It's solving the "I don't trust the app" thing. That's the big one for me. The not-trying part, I think, is anything during the stay. Like it's not babysitting the property. It's a check before the guest comes in and then it gets out of the way. Which honestly is fine with me. I don't want more notifications.

**Mike:** What does it not do that you'd have expected it to do?

**Host A:** Hmm. I kind of expected it to be able to send me a battery prediction. Like, "you have eight more check-ins before this dies." Because right now the percentage is meaningless. If it can't tell me when the battery is actually going to fail, then it's still going to surprise me eventually.

**Mike:** Fair. The "ready" message. Walk me through what you'd want it to say, when, and how you'd want to get it.

**Host A:** Short. Really short. Like, "Ready for [guest name], 4pm." That's it. Push notification, on my phone. Not a text, I get too many texts. Not email, I'd never see it. Push, two hours before check-in like you said, one line. If it's longer than one line I'm not going to read it because I'll assume nothing's wrong.

**Mike:** And the "not ready" message?

**Host A:** That one I want to actually read. So it should be different. Like, different sound on the phone if that's possible. And the message should tell me three things. What's wrong, like "battery low" or "code didn't sync." What it means for the guest, like will they be locked out or is this just a warning. And what I need to do, like "go change batteries" or "the system will retry in 15 minutes, no action needed." If you can't give me action steps I'm just going to panic.

**Mike:** The product won't unlock doors remotely. Reaction?

**Host A:** Totally fine. I don't want it to. The remote unlock today only works half the time anyway, and honestly if my product is unlocking my front door from the cloud I'd rather it not. That feels like the kind of thing that goes wrong at 2am and ends up on the news. Keep it out.

## Section 5 — Usage intent and feature reactions

**Mike:** Imagine this works as described. How does your week change?

**Host A:** I sleep on Thursday night. That's how it changes. Right now I'm anxious every check-in day and if this thing actually tells me with confidence that the lock is ready, I get those hours back. It's not a money thing for me, it's a quality of life thing.

**Mike:** Which part matters most? Pre-arrival check, code automation, cleaner rotation?

**Host A:** Pre-arrival check, by a mile. The code automation is nice but I've gotten okay at the copy-paste thing. The check is the part I literally cannot do myself, because the app doesn't tell me the truth. The cleaner rotation, eh, I'll talk about that one.

**Mike:** What's missing that would stop you from using it?

**Host A:** If it doesn't work with my Wi-Fi situation, dead on arrival. My mesh network has a 2.4 and a 5 and the lock needs to be on the 2.4 and the app has been a disaster about that. If this new thing is built on the same broken plumbing I'm not going to magically trust it more. So I'd want to know it's actually been tested on weird home Wi-Fi.

**Mike:** If you were the PM and had to ship a v1, what would you cut?

**Host A:** Cut the cleaner rotation. Or at least make it opt-in and off by default. Ship the pre-arrival check and the guest code stuff first. Get me to trust that, then maybe I'll let you mess with my cleaner.

**Mike:** Van Westendorp, four quick prices. At what monthly price is this too expensive to even consider?

**Host A:** Thirty bucks a month. If you told me thirty I'd close the tab.

**Mike:** At what price is it expensive but you'd think about it?

**Host A:** Maybe fifteen. I'd think about it at fifteen but I'd probably ask around on Reddit first to see if anyone is actually getting their money's worth.

**Mike:** Great deal?

**Host A:** Seven. Like seven, eight bucks a month. That's a Netflix-low subscription where I'm just like, fine, take it.

**Mike:** So cheap you'd question whether it works?

**Host A:** Under three. If it's two bucks a month I'm wondering what the catch is. Like, am I the product, are you selling my lock data, something's off.

**Mike:** Cleaner code rotation. Would you trust the system to revoke a cleaner's code automatically after her window ends, without you confirming?

**Host A:** No. Not at first. My cleaner has had the same code for almost two years and she's part of how this place runs. If the system rotates her code on a Tuesday and she shows up Wednesday and can't get in and I'm in a meeting, that's a disaster. I'd want to opt into that, and I'd want a heads-up the day before, and I'd want to be able to just pause it for her specifically. Honestly, I might just leave it off.

**Mike:** If the system said everything was ready, and a guest got locked out anyway. Reaction?

**Host A:** Furious. Probably stop using it for a month. But, and this is the honest answer, if it had been right the previous twenty times I'd come back. One failure I can rationalize. Two in a row and I'm out.

**Mike:** First 30 days, how much would you trust?

**Host A:** Probably 60 percent. I'd let it do the check and the guest codes. I would not let it touch the cleaner code. I'd watch the alerts for a month and if they were accurate I'd loosen up.

## Section 6 — Anti-goal stress test

**Mike:** No auto-extending a guest code if you let them stay an extra night. Bothers you, or feels right?

**Host A:** Feels right. I want to make that call. If I'm extending a stay I'm probably also charging them and I'm doing it in Airbnb anyway, so the code thing should follow that. I don't want the system guessing.

**Mike:** No messaging guests directly. If you did build that, would you use it?

**Host A:** Honestly maybe, but I'd be nervous. My guest messaging is in Airbnb because that's where the record is if there's a dispute. If Schlage starts texting my guest and the guest replies and I never see it, that's bad. So if you built it, I'd want every message copied to me, and I'd want the guest's reply to go to me, not into some Schlage support bucket. If you can promise that, fine. Otherwise leave it alone.

**Mike:** No after-stay surveillance reporting. Change how you think about the product?

**Host A:** No, that's a feature for me. I don't want to know who came in after checkout. If I see in some log that the cleaner brought a friend, what am I supposed to do with that? Now I have a problem I didn't have. Keep it out.

**Mike:** When you picked your current lock, what else did you look at?

**Host A:** I looked at August briefly. I think I looked at Yale too. I ended up on Schlage because someone on r/airbnb_hosts said it was the most reliable for short-term rentals and I trusted that more than the marketing on the brand sites. Honestly I'd go back to Reddit before I'd believe any of this.

**Mike:** If a Schlage rep tried to sell you this tomorrow, what's the hardest objection for them to answer?

**Host A:** Why should I believe this app is going to work when the current app is the whole reason I'm in this mess? Like, the app is the broken part. If your new product is built on top of the same app that lies to me about battery levels, why is bolting AI on top going to fix that? Fix the app first. That's the question I'd ask and I don't think they'd have a good answer.

**Mike:** Anything we didn't ask?

**Host A:** Just, please make the alerts useful. If I get an alert that says "lock issue detected" and nothing else I will scream. Tell me what's wrong, tell me what to do, and please please please don't send me ten of them.
