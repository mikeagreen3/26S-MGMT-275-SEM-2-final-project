# Host B — Raw Interview Transcript

## Section 1 — Warm-up and background

**Mike:** Thanks for making time. To get us going, tell me a bit about your hosting situation. How many places, where, how long?

**Host B:** It's one place. Just the one. It's a beach house, and honestly we're still pretty new at this. Less than two years, I'd say. My husband and I are doing this kind of half on purpose, half by accident? We bought the house thinking we'd use it ourselves, and then the math only worked if we rented it. So now we rent it.

**Mike:** Got it. Which platforms?

**Host B:** Airbnb. Only Airbnb. I know people say put it everywhere, but I can barely keep up with one calendar.

**Mike:** Walk me through your operating model. Are you solo, do you have a cleaner, a co-host?

**Host B:** Solo, basically. I have a cleaner who does turnovers, she's local, she's great, but she's the only other person involved. No co-host, no property manager. I'm the one answering messages at midnight. My husband helps with bigger stuff like, you know, when the dishwasher dies, but the day-to-day is me.

**Mike:** And the lock setup?

**Host B:** It's a Schlage Encode on the front door. Just the one lock, one door. We installed it maybe a year and a half ago when we first listed.

**Mike:** Okay. Walk me through how front-door access works for a booking, from the moment somebody books to the moment they leave. Don't shortcut, I want every step.

**Host B:** Sure. So a booking comes in on Airbnb. I get the notification. I usually wait until a day or two before check-in, and then I go into the Schlage app and I add a code for them. I use the last four of their phone number, that's my little system. Then I message them on Airbnb with the code and the check-in instructions, and I tell them the lockbox is on the side of the house if for any reason the code doesn't work, and the key inside the lockbox is the backup. They check in, they stay, they check out. Then I'll go in the app a couple days later and delete the code. Sometimes I forget and there are a lot of old codes sitting in there, which I know is bad but it hasn't bitten me yet.

## Section 2 — Problem validation

**Mike:** Tell me about the last time front-door access did not go smoothly.

**Host B:** Oh, you want the whole story? Okay. It was last winter, and we had this storm system that came through. Just rain and rain and rain. And we had a family checking in. Two adults, two kids, one of them a baby. They get to the house and the lock just doesn't work. Doesn't respond. They try the code, nothing. They try the lockbox, and I had forgotten, I had literally forgotten to put the spare key back in the lockbox the last time I'd used it. So the lockbox was just empty. And I'm ninety minutes away.

**Mike:** How'd you find out?

**Host B:** The mom called me. She was as polite as a person can be while standing in the rain with a baby. I'll never forget it. I was making dinner and my phone rang and I could hear the wind through the phone. That was the moment I learned what condensation does to electronics, by the way.

**Mike:** What did you do?

**Host B:** I got in the car. There's not much else to do. I drove the ninety minutes, in the rain, with my husband texting me ideas the whole way, none of which I could actually do from the driver's seat. By the time I got there it had been an hour and a half. They were huddled on the porch under a beach towel. I let them in with my own key, I apologized about forty times, I gave them a partial refund out of my own pocket, I left them a bottle of wine and a fruit basket the next morning.

**Mike:** What did it cost you beyond the time and the refund?

**Host B:** Sleep, mostly. I didn't sleep for like a week. Every time I'd close my eyes I'd picture that baby in the rain. The review was actually generous, four stars, she even said in the body of it that I'd handled it well once I got there. But I know what that does to my listing. I dropped pages in search. And the lock itself, the locksmith said it had water inside it. Both locks, actually, we have two Encodes and the back one had the same problem, just nobody was trying to use it that day.

**Mike:** Set aside the major failures. On a normal week, what's the most annoying part of access management?

**Host B:** Honestly the codes piling up. I look at the app and there's like fourteen old codes in there and I don't remember who any of them are. That, and the battery anxiety. I look at the battery percentage probably twice a day. I'm not exaggerating. Twice a day. It's a tic at this point.

**Mike:** How do you handle codes for the cleaner?

**Host B:** She has a permanent code. Same one for like a year. I know I should rotate it. But she's been with me forever and I trust her and the thought of one more thing to manage makes me want to lie down. So it's permanent.

**Mike:** Hypothetically, if you knew there was a fifty percent chance the lock would fail at tonight's check-in, what would you actually do right now?

**Host B:** Drive there. Today. Right now. I'd cancel my afternoon and drive. Or I'd call my neighbor down the road and beg her to go check the lockbox and put a key in it and physically meet the guest. Fifty percent? I'm not gambling on fifty percent with a baby in the rain again.

## Section 3 — Concept presentation

**Mike:** Okay, let me read you a concept and then we'll talk about it. Schlage AI Access Concierge is a software update for Schlage Encode and Encode Plus locks. About two hours before every check-in, it runs an automatic readiness check on each of your locks. It looks at battery, Wi-Fi connection, whether the guest's code actually synced from your booking platform, and whether the code is physically present on the lock. If everything passes, you get one short message that says you're ready. If something fails, you get a specific alert that tells you what's wrong and what to do next.

The system also handles your guest and cleaner code lifecycle on confirmed bookings. When a booking comes in, the guest code is created and pushed to the lock automatically. After a cleaner's window ends, their code rotates. At checkout, the guest code is revoked. No manual code entry.

A few things it specifically does not do: it won't unlock doors remotely. It won't message your guests directly. It won't auto-extend a code if a guest stays past checkout. And it won't watch the property during the stay. One check per booking, before arrival.

**Host B:** Okay. Read that to me one more time?

**Mike:** Sure. [Re-reads the concept.]

**Host B:** Okay. I have a question. The check, two hours before. Does it actually try the code or does it just look at the lock and see that a code exists in it?

**Mike:** It checks that the code is physically present on the lock, not just that the booking system thinks it sent it.

**Host B:** Okay. And does it know if the lock is wet?

**Mike:** It can tell you about battery and connectivity, and whether the code is there. It doesn't have a moisture sensor. So if water has gotten inside but the lock still reports normally, it wouldn't catch that.

**Host B:** Mm.

## Section 4 — Solution comprehension

**Mike:** In your own words, what is this?

**Host B:** It's a babysitter for the lock. It checks the lock before the guest gets there so I don't have to. And it manages the codes so I don't have to remember to add and delete them.

**Mike:** What problem is it solving, and what is it not trying to solve?

**Host B:** It's solving "is the lock going to work when the guest pulls up." It's not solving "the lock is broken." Right? If the lock is dead, this thing doesn't fix it. It just tells me earlier.

**Mike:** That's right.

**Host B:** That matters a lot for me actually. Because my failure mode is hardware. My locks have died from weather. This doesn't stop that. It just gives me a head start.

**Mike:** What does it not do that you would have expected it to?

**Host B:** I expected it to do something about the weather. Like, I don't know, "your humidity has been high for three days, you should go check on it." That'd be amazing. But it sounds like it's just doing the two-hours-before thing.

**Mike:** Correct.

**Host B:** Okay.

**Mike:** The "ready" message. Walk me through what you'd want it to say, when, and how.

**Host B:** Short. Please. I don't need a paragraph. Something like "Beach house is ready for the Smiths, 3pm check-in." That's it. And I want it as a text message. Not in an app. I don't want to open an app to find out my lock is fine. Text me. Two hours before is good. Maybe three. Two feels close.

**Mike:** And the "not ready" message?

**Host B:** Totally different. Loud. I want to know what's wrong, where the lock is in the failure, and what I'm supposed to do. So like, "Battery at 8%, replace before 3pm" or "Code didn't push to the lock, here's the workaround." Tell me the action. Don't make me guess. And for the love of god, do not send me a "not ready" alert that just says "something's wrong, check the app." That would make my eye twitch.

**Mike:** It does not unlock doors remotely. Reaction?

**Host B:** Fine. Honestly that's fine. I don't want to unlock the door from ninety minutes away. If a guest can't get in I want to know why, not paper over it by buzzing them in like they're in an apartment building. And if Schlage could remotely unlock my door, somebody else could remotely unlock my door, and that's a whole thing I don't want to think about.

## Section 5 — Usage intent and feature reactions

**Mike:** Imagine this works exactly as described. How does your week change?

**Host B:** I check the battery percentage less. Maybe. Eventually. I think the first few months I'd still check obsessively because I wouldn't trust the system yet. But if it works, if the two-hours-before text actually shows up and is accurate, I think I'd sleep better on check-in nights. That's the main thing. I'd sleep.

**Mike:** Which part matters most? Pre-arrival check, code automation, cleaner rotation?

**Host B:** Pre-arrival check. By a mile. The code thing is nice. The cleaner thing I'm not sure I want. But the pre-arrival check is the whole reason I'd consider this.

**Mike:** What's missing that would stop you from using it?

**Host B:** A "the lock is wet" sensor. I know that's not what this is. But that's my fear. My fear isn't that I forgot to push a code. My fear is the hardware dying in weather.

**Mike:** If you were the PM and had to ship the simplest v1, what would you cut?

**Host B:** Cut the cleaner rotation. Or make it opt-in. I don't need it. The guest code revoke at checkout, fine, keep that. But rotating my cleaner's code without me saying so? Cut it for v1.

**Mike:** Van Westendorp pricing. Four quick ones. At what monthly price would this feel too expensive to even consider?

**Host B:** Forty dollars a month. If you told me forty I'd laugh.

**Mike:** Expensive but you'd still think about it?

**Host B:** Twenty-five.

**Mike:** Great deal?

**Host B:** Ten. Ten a month I'd sign up tonight.

**Mike:** So cheap you'd question whether it works?

**Host B:** Three. If it's three dollars a month I'm wondering what the catch is.

**Mike:** The cleaner code rotation. Would you trust the system to revoke your cleaner's code automatically after her window ends, without you confirming?

**Host B:** No. I mean, not at first. Listen, my cleaner has been with me forever. If the system decides her window has ended and locks her out, and she shows up to do a turnover and can't get in, that is a disaster for me. She's not gonna sit in her car and wait for me to figure it out. She's gonna leave, and then I have a dirty house and a guest coming. So no. I want to confirm. Or I want the cleaner code to be left alone entirely and I'll manage it myself, like I do today.

**Mike:** If the system told you everything looked ready, and then a guest got locked out anyway, what happens to your trust?

**Host B:** That'd be really bad. I don't know if it ends it. Once, maybe, I'd want to know what happened and whether it was a one-off. Twice and I'm done. I'd rather have no system and check it myself than a system that lied to me. Lying is worse than not knowing.

**Mike:** How much would you trust in the first 30 days?

**Host B:** Almost none. I'd be running the system in parallel with my own checking. I'd be in the app twice a day still. I'd treat its messages as a second opinion, not the source of truth. After 30 days, if it'd been right every time, I'd probably start to let go a little.

## Section 6 — Anti-goal stress test

**Mike:** It won't auto-extend a guest's code if you let them stay an extra night. Does that bother you or feel right?

**Host B:** Feels right. I want to be the one who says yes to an extension. That's a money conversation and a trust conversation. I don't want a system making that call.

**Mike:** It won't message your guest directly, even check-in instructions. If we did build that, would you use it?

**Host B:** No. Or, I don't know, maybe? Honestly the messaging is the one place I feel in control. I write to my guests like a human. I tell them about the good coffee shop. If Schlage sent a robot message saying "your code is 1234," that's not me, that's a corporation, and the guest is going to feel that. Don't build it. Or build it as a fallback that I can turn on if my hair is on fire, but default off.

**Mike:** It won't show you who entered the property after a stay ended. No after-stay surveillance reporting. Does that change how you think about the product?

**Host B:** I hadn't even thought about that. I don't want that. I don't want to know. If the stay's over, it's over. I don't need a log of who came in after, that feels creepy, and also it would just give me one more thing to worry about. Leave that out.

## Section 7 — Wrap and comparison

**Mike:** When you picked your current lock, what alternatives did you consider?

**Host B:** Honestly not many. I looked at August and Yale a little. I ended up with Schlage because BiggerPockets people swore by the Encode. That's where I get most of my advice. The forum, basically. I trust that crowd more than I trust reviews on Amazon.

**Mike:** If a Schlage rep walked into your kitchen tomorrow and tried to sell you this, what's the one objection you'd raise that would be hardest for them to answer?

**Host B:** The hardest one? My locks died from water. Your software update isn't going to keep my locks alive in a storm. You're telling me earlier that they're dying, but you're not stopping it. Why am I paying you a monthly fee for an earlier warning when what I actually need is a lock that survives the coast? Sell me the lock that doesn't die. Then we can talk about the software.

**Mike:** Anything we didn't ask that you'd want us to know?

**Host B:** Yeah. The lockbox. The physical lockbox with the physical key. Whatever you build, do not make me feel like I can stop using that. I will always have the lockbox. I will always have the key. If your product is built on the assumption that the smart lock is the only way in, you're going to scare me off. The smart lock is the front door. The lockbox is the parachute. Don't touch my parachute.
