# Host D — The August Agonizer — Synthesis

## Anonymized profile

Single-unit urban Airbnb host, 10+ years experience. Solo operator with one long-tenured cleaner. Currently runs a competitor smart lock (August with keypad) and has manually disabled the platform integration after a year of intermittent failures. Medium tech savviness, high pain tolerance, and a deep trust scar around automation that was supposed to "just work."

## Top 3 pains

1. **Silent code-sync failures.** The booking platform integration would push codes that never landed on the lock, or pushed the wrong digits, and the host had no signal until the guest messaged at check-in. "The system doesn't tell me anything is wrong. It just silently fails."
2. **Manual code generation as the workaround tax.** After losing trust, the host now generates every guest code by hand. "It takes me less than a minute per guest. But it's a minute I have to do every single time, and it's a context switch."
3. **Loss of sleep over late-night check-in risk.** Even when failures are recoverable, the cost shows up as ambient anxiety on arrival nights. "I sat up for another hour after she got in because I was worried she'd message again."

## Reactions per capability

- **Pre-arrival readiness check:** Strongest pull. The exact problem they have. Would buy v1 on this feature alone.
- **Code automation on booking:** Wanted, but not trusted on day one. Would run in parallel with manual codes for at least a month.
- **Cleaner code rotation:** Hard no on autopilot. Cleaner relationship is too high-stakes to delegate to software. Requires explicit confirmation step or the feature is dead-on-arrival.
- **Anti-goal — no remote unlock:** Understood, doesn't love it. The emergency-tool gap is real.
- **Anti-goal — no guest messaging:** Strongly endorsed. Host voice is part of their brand.
- **Anti-goal — no after-stay surveillance:** Strongly endorsed. Finds the alternative creepy.
- **Anti-goal — no auto-extend:** Endorsed. Wants deliberate human decision every time.

## Standout quotes

- "This is what August's integration was supposed to be, and that one ruined my life."
- "Cut the cleaner rotation. Cut the code revoke at checkout. Just do the pre-arrival check. That's the wedge."
- "I'm not an early adopter for software that touches my front door. Not anymore."
- "I've already lived through the August integration. I'm not living through a second betrayal of the same shape."

## Van Westendorp pricing (per month, single unit)

- **Too expensive:** $20+
- **Expensive but considering:** $12–15
- **Great deal:** $5–7
- **Too cheap (suspicious):** under $2

Narrow band. Reflects single-unit economics and skepticism that cheap software gets maintained.

## Killer objection

"I'd have to rip out my August lock and buy your hardware to use your software. Why would I trust your integration when the last one I trusted made me manually run codes for a year? Prove to me your software is fundamentally different, not just newer."

Two-layer objection. The brand-switch cost is the obvious half. The harder half is the trust scar — they've been burned by exactly this product shape before, and "newer" is not a sufficient answer. Schlage has to convince them twice: once on product quality, once on the cost of disposable hardware.

## What surprised us

- They volunteered "skin in the game" pricing unprompted. They want Schlage to back the readiness check with some form of guarantee (month credit, refund cover) and explicitly said this is what would separate Schlage from the August experience.
- They asked for an on-demand "test the lock now" button. The two-hour pre-arrival check feels right, but they want a manual re-run as a control surface, especially as the guest gets close.
- The most-loved feature is also the most-feared. The readiness check is exactly the pain, but it's also the exact failure mode that broke their trust before. A false-positive readiness signal is a brand-killing event for this segment.
- Their preference for the "ready" message is minimalist ("just tell me I'm clear, no checklist"), but the "not ready" message wants everything: what failed, which lock, what action, time remaining, and a one-click resolution path inside the notification.

## Brand-switch signal

**Soft yes, with conditions.** They would consider switching off August for this software, but the bar is high.

- **Threshold 1 — proof of stability:** Six months of independent host reports (Reddit, App Store) confirming the readiness check doesn't false-positive. Not a demo. Not a marketing case study. Forum signal.
- **Threshold 2 — accuracy guarantee:** Some form of financial skin in the game when the readiness check is wrong. "Sorry, we'll do better" already failed them once.
- **Threshold 3 — hardware migration cost:** They'd need the total switching cost (Encode hardware + install + software subscription) to come in under what they value the anxiety reduction at, roughly $5–7/month plus a one-time hardware hit they'd absorb only if thresholds 1 and 2 are met.

Strategically: this persona represents a real but slow-moving switching pool. They are unlikely to be early adopters. They are likely to convert in year two or three if the early Encode cohort posts positive forum signal. For Schlage, that means the launch wedge should target greenfield buyers and Encode upgraders first, and segments like this should be served by a delayed marketing motion built on social proof rather than direct outbound.
