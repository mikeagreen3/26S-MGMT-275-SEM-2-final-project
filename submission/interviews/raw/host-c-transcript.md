# Host C — Raw Interview Transcript

## Section 1 — Warm-up and background

**Mike:** Thanks for taking the time. Let's start easy. Tell me a bit about your hosting situation. How many properties, where, how long.

**Host C:** One unit. Just the one. I'm pretty new at this, less than a couple years in. I'd rather not get too specific on location, but it's not in a city. Weather here is part of why we're having this conversation, I'll get to that.

**Mike:** Got it. Which booking platforms are you on?

**Host C:** Airbnb. Just Airbnb. People keep telling me to go on VRBO too, and maybe I will, but I've got enough to keep track of with one platform. I'm not somebody who's running ten units off a dashboard. I'm one guy with one rental.

**Mike:** Operating model. Solo, cleaner, co-host?

**Host C:** Solo. I do the turns myself when I can. When I can't, I've got a woman down the road who helps, and she's good, but she's not on a payroll or anything. I pay her cash for the job. There's no app between us. She texts me when she's done.

**Mike:** Your lock setup. What's on the door right now?

**Host C:** Kwikset SmartCode 270. Keypad deadbolt, no Wi-Fi, no app, no nothing. Just a keypad. I punch in a code, I change the code when I need to change the code, that's it. I've also got a combo lockbox on the side of the house with a backup key in it for when something goes sideways.

**Mike:** And before that?

**Host C:** Before that I had one of the connected ones. I'm not going to name and shame, but it was one of the big brands. I'd be the one to tell you it was great when it worked. The trouble was it didn't always work. So I took it off and put the Kwikset on, and honest to god, I've slept better since.

**Mike:** Walk me through how access works for a typical booking, start to finish.

**Host C:** Sure. Booking comes in on Airbnb. I send them a message a couple days out, just a normal one, here's the address, here's parking, check-in is after 3. Day of, I go over to the lock, I punch in my master code, I program their code. Four digits. Last four of their phone number usually, easy to remember. I text them the code separately, not through Airbnb messages, just a text. Then they show up and they get in. Day after they leave, I drive over, change the code to the next person's, or back to a default if there's a gap. Done.

**Mike:** You drive over for every turn?

**Host C:** Every turn. It's ten minutes. I'm there for the cleaning anyway most of the time. It is not a hardship.

## Section 2 — Problem validation

**Mike:** Tell me about the last time front-door access didn't go smoothly.

**Host C:** This was back on the smart lock, not the Kwikset. We had a stretch of storms, real bad ones. Wi-Fi was in and out for two days. I had a guest checking in at 4 in the afternoon, and the code I'd pushed from the app hadn't synced. So they got there, punched in the code I'd given them, lock said no. They called me. I had to drive over in the rain, climb up onto the porch, manually program a code on the lock with the master code I thankfully still had written down. Took me forty minutes, between the drive and the fiddling. Guest was nice about it. But that was the moment I knew I was done with the connected stuff.

**Mike:** How'd you find out something was wrong? Guest or you?

**Host C:** Guest called me. The app didn't tell me anything was wrong. The app told me everything was fine, actually. It thought the code was on the lock. It wasn't. That's the part that really got me. The system was lying to me, in a way. Not on purpose. But the dashboard said one thing and the lock said another. So which one am I supposed to trust?

**Mike:** What did that cost you, beyond the time?

**Host C:** It cost me trust. I didn't get a bad review out of it, the guest was a sweetheart about the whole thing. But I sat in my truck after I left there and I thought, what if she'd been somebody else? What if it'd been somebody with a kid in the car at 10 at night? I just kept playing it forward. And I thought, I'm not going to be the guy whose technology fails on a family in the rain. I'd rather drive over for every single turn.

**Mike:** On a normal week, set aside the disasters, what's the most annoying part of access management?

**Host C:** Honest answer? Not much. With the Kwikset, it's pretty quiet. The annoying part is when somebody messages me at 11 at night to ask me what the code is, even though I sent it to them twice. Or when they say the code doesn't work and what they actually did is they typed it wrong. People do that. That's not a lock problem though. That's a people problem. No software's going to fix that.

**Mike:** Cleaners and maintenance, how do you handle codes?

**Host C:** She's got a code that I don't change. It's her code. She's the only one who has it besides me. I trust her. If she ever stopped working with me, I'd change it the day she finished. But she's been with me the whole time. Why would I rotate her code every week? I know her.

**Mike:** Hypothetically, if you knew the lock would fail at tonight's check-in with 50% odds, what would you do right now?

**Host C:** I'd drive over right now and put a key in the lockbox and tell the guest the code to the lockbox. Belt and suspenders. I'm not gambling on a coin flip with somebody's vacation.

## Section 4 — Solution comprehension

**Mike:** In your own words, what is this product?

**Host C:** It's a babysitter for your smart lock. It's a piece of software that watches your lock for you, two hours before somebody shows up, and tells you if the lock is still alive. And it also pushes codes around for you so you don't have to type them in. That about right?

**Mike:** That's about right.

**Host C:** Okay. So my first reaction, and I'll just say it, is that this product exists because the underlying thing doesn't work. You're selling me a piece of software to check on a piece of hardware that's supposed to already work. If the lock worked, you wouldn't need the babysitter. That's where my head goes.

**Mike:** What problem do you think it's meant to solve, and what's it not trying to solve?

**Host C:** It's meant to solve the panic of not knowing if your lock is ready. Which is a real panic, I had it. It is not trying to solve the actual lock failing. That's still your problem. It's just going to tell you sooner.

**Mike:** What does it not do that you'd have expected it to?

**Host C:** I'd have expected it to fix the lock. Or at least try. But you told me it doesn't unlock the door, doesn't message the guest, doesn't extend the code. So really all it's doing is watching and telling. Which is fine I guess. But it's not solving anything, it's just notifying me earlier. I want to be clear about that distinction.

**Mike:** The "ready" message. What would you want it to say, when, how?

**Host C:** I don't want it. I don't want a "ready" message. If everything is fine, don't tell me. That's the whole point of fine. I get enough notifications. The minute you start sending me a message every time a guest is coming, telling me the lock is okay, I'm going to start ignoring all of them. And then the one time it actually matters, I'm going to miss it. So the "ready" message should not exist. Send me the "not ready" message. That's the only one I need.

**Mike:** Got it. What about the "not ready" message?

**Host C:** That one I need. And I need it to tell me exactly what's wrong, in plain English. Not "code sync error 4035." Tell me, the code isn't on the lock. Tell me, the battery is below 20 percent. Tell me, the Wi-Fi has been off for an hour. And tell me what to do next. And I want it as a text message, not a notification in some app I'm going to forget to open. Text me. Like a human would.

**Mike:** The product doesn't unlock doors remotely. Reaction?

**Host C:** Good. That's the one thing I like so far. I don't want anybody, including Schlage, including your software, including some AI, being able to open my front door from the internet. That's a non-starter for me. The fact that you decided not to do that is the only reason I'm still in this conversation, honestly.

## Section 5 — Usage intent and feature reactions

**Mike:** Imagine this works exactly as described. How does your week change?

**Host C:** It doesn't, much. Because I don't have a connected lock. So step one for me to use this is, I throw away my Kwikset and I buy a Schlage Encode. Which is a couple hundred bucks. And then I plug it into Wi-Fi and hope my Wi-Fi doesn't go out. So your product is asking me to take on the exact problem I removed from my life two years ago, in exchange for a notification when that problem comes back. I don't see the trade.

**Mike:** Which part matters most, the pre-arrival check, the code automation, the cleaner rotation?

**Host C:** None of it. If I had to pick, the pre-arrival check is the least offensive. The code automation, I can do that myself in 30 seconds with a keypad. The cleaner rotation actively bothers me. I trust my cleaner. I don't need a piece of software rotating her code every Tuesday like she's a security risk.

**Mike:** What's missing that would stop you from using it?

**Host C:** A version that doesn't need Wi-Fi or an app or a monthly fee. That's what's missing. Build me that and we'll talk.

**Mike:** If you had to ship the simplest possible v1, what would you cut?

**Host C:** Cut the "ready" message, like I said. Cut the cleaner rotation, leave that to the host. Cut the code automation if you can, or at least make it optional. Keep the failure alert. That's the only thing in this whole product that's actually doing work.

**Mike:** Pricing, four quick ones. What monthly price would feel too expensive to even consider?

**Host C:** Anything. I'm not paying a monthly fee for a lock. I'll say it twice. Not a dollar a month, not five dollars a month, not fifty. Subscriptions on physical hardware I already bought are a racket. I bought the lock. The lock is mine. The fact that you want to charge me every month for it to work is the entire reason I don't have one.

**Mike:** So what about expensive but you'd think about it?

**Host C:** Same answer. I'm not thinking about it.

**Mike:** A great deal?

**Host C:** Free, included with the lock, no subscription. That's the deal. Charge me more for the lock if you have to. I'd rather pay 300 dollars once than 5 dollars a month forever.

**Mike:** So cheap you'd question whether it works?

**Host C:** Any subscription at all makes me question whether it works. Because if it worked, you wouldn't need the recurring revenue. You'd just sell me the lock.

**Mike:** Cleaner code rotation. Trust the system to revoke automatically?

**Host C:** No. Absolutely not. And I'll tell you why. My cleaner shows up sometimes the day after checkout because something needed extra attention. Sometimes she comes back the next morning because she forgot a rag. If your system has decided her window is over and her code is dead, she's standing on my porch in the cold calling me, and now I've got to drive over. You're solving a problem I don't have by creating a problem I will have. Let the host decide when the cleaner's code is dead. We know our people. You don't.

**Mike:** If the system told you everything was ready, and a guest still got locked out, what happens?

**Host C:** That's the whole thing right there. That's the moment. That's what happened to me on the old smart lock, the dashboard said go, the lock said no. If your product does that, I'm done. Not just with the product, with the brand. Because what you've sold me is peace of mind, and the one job of peace of mind is to not lie. If you tell me I'm ready and I'm not, I will never trust another green checkmark from you again. And I'll tell every host I know on Airhosts Forum about it.

**Mike:** First 30 days, how much do you trust it?

**Host C:** Zero percent. I'd be checking every code myself by hand for a month. Maybe two. And if I'm doing that anyway, what am I paying you for?

## Section 6 — Anti-goal stress test

**Mike:** Won't auto-extend a guest's code if you let them stay an extra night. Bother you?

**Host C:** That's fine. Hosts should be in the loop on extensions anyway. The lock isn't the problem there, the money is. I'd want to talk to the guest before extending anything. Don't make the lock do that automatically.

**Mike:** Won't message the guest directly. If you did build it, would you use it?

**Host C:** No. I message my guests myself, like a human being. The whole reason somebody books a small place from a small host is they want a person on the other end. The minute Schlage starts talking to my guests, I'm the third party in my own rental. That's backwards.

**Mike:** Won't show who entered after a stay ended, no after-stay surveillance. Change anything?

**Host C:** I don't want surveillance reports. I don't want a list of who punched in what code at what time after the stay. That feels like the kind of thing that lives on a server somewhere, and one day there's a breach, and now my guests are in a database. No thank you. Glad you're not building it.

## Section 7 — Wrap and comparison

**Mike:** When you picked the Kwikset, what else did you look at?

**Host C:** I looked at going back to a regular deadbolt with a real key and a real lockbox. Almost did it. The only reason I went with the Kwikset is the keypad part is convenient for guests. I considered nothing with Wi-Fi. Nothing on an app. Nothing requiring a phone. I read a long thread on Airhosts Forum about people getting burned on the connected ones, and that sealed it.

**Mike:** If a Schlage rep walked into your kitchen tomorrow with this, what's the one objection that'd be hardest for them to answer?

**Host C:** Why am I paying you every month to tell me whether the thing I already bought is working. That's the question. If the lock is good, I shouldn't need a babysitter. If the lock needs a babysitter, the lock isn't good. Answer that one and I'll listen to the rest.

**Mike:** Anything we didn't ask?

**Host C:** Yeah, one thing. If you're going to build this anyway, and I know you are, the only version of it I'd ever consider is one where the failure alert works without Wi-Fi. Cellular backup, something. Because the whole reason the smart lock failed me last time was Wi-Fi. So if your check-in monitor depends on the same Wi-Fi that's going to fail, you've built a smoke alarm that turns off in a fire. Think about that one.

**Mike:** Fair point. Thanks for the time.

**Host C:** Anytime.
